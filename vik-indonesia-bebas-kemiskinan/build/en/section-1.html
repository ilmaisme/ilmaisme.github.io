Tirta and Emiliana

Tirta means water. Emiliana is a girl from Ende, East Nusa Tenggara. Though Tirta and Emiliana are close to one another, they feel far apart. No pipe connects them. For something as basic as drinking and bathing, Emiliana and her friends must walk kilometers, carrying jerrycans with both hands. At school, her mind is often less on books, but more on whether she’ll meet Tirta along the way.
Sadly, Emiliana’s story is not unique. Even in West Kalimantan, home to 8.3 million hectares of forest, finding Tirta is a struggle. Look at the map, and you’ll see, not everyone in Indonesia has access to clean water. The cost isn’t only thirst, but also poor sanitation and failing health.

Clean water and sanitation for NTT and Kalimantan Barat.

And that’s already the best case. Those numbers reflect only “basic” access. If we talk about safe access, the picture is far worse.
In 2024, the Ministry of Public Works reported that households with safe drinking water were only 30.96% in West Kalimantan, 25.38% in NTT. In addition, households with safe sanitation were just 3.52% in West Kalimantan, 2.44% in NTT.
Often, water scarcity is blamed only on drought. But the data shows that the real issue is funding.
